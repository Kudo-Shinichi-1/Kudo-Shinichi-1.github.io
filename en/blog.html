About My Blog

These are the articles, reports, and shares I have written in recent years, reflecting my daily academic thoughts and reflections, mainly focused on discussions of English and American literature and pedagogy. I believe these academic explorations are valuable, and I share them here with teachers and friends around me.

For reprints of the original content, please cite the source.


2023

  • Exploring the Impact of Kinesthetic Experiences and Spatial Metaphors in Teaching Practice on Second Grade Students' Comprehension of Abstract Verbs (On Children's Language Acquisition)
    This is a long article that explores the effectiveness of two cognitive theories—embodied cognition theory and conceptual metaphor theory—when teaching abstract verbs. By incorporating kinesthetic experiences and spatial metaphors into teaching, it evaluates how these innovative methods impact second-grade students' understanding of abstract verbs, aiming to improve language learning outcomes and promote children's language and cognitive development. (Co-author: Xia Hongyu)

  • Report: The item-based nature of children's early syntactic development Slides | Handouts (On Children's Language Acquisition)
    Children learn concrete language expressions by imitating the language they hear around them and then use their general cognitive and social cognitive skills to categorize, generalize, and creatively combine these expressions and structures. Where does grammatical structure come from? In general, we can assume that human language ultimately originated from a species-specific biological adaptation for symbolic communication, but the actual grammatical structures of modern language were created by humans through grammaticalization processes in specific cultural histories, as well as through cultural learning, schema formation, and structural combination in individual development.

  • A Comparative Analysis of Tragic Romance in The Scarlet Letter and A Rose for Emily (On American Literature)
    This article explores the tragic romance of the female protagonists Hester Prynne from Nathaniel Hawthorne's *The Scarlet Letter* and Emily Grierson from William Faulkner's *A Rose for Emily*. It aims to explain the common elements and unique features in their love stories. The analysis reveals the profound effects of social constraints, differing views on love, and their respective coping mechanisms.

  • A Comparative Analysis of the Symbol of the Clothing in The Emperor's New Clothes and The Happy Man's Shirt (On Western Children's Literature)
    This article compares the Western children's literature works *The Emperor's New Clothes* and *The Happy Man's Shirt*, focusing on the themes of power, desire, materialism, ideological oppression, and resistance conveyed through clothing as a symbolic medium. I analyze the vivid metaphors and social critiques in these stories, uncovering the profound moral messages hidden beneath seemingly simple plots.

  • The development and predicament of Chinese sign language (On Linguistics)
    Discussing the standardization process of Chinese sign language and the challenges it faces, analyzing the differences between natural sign language and sign Chinese, as well as the application challenges in deaf education. Although sign language has made some progress in standardization, there are still linguistic differences in practical teaching. It is recommended that future education focus more on the needs of deaf students and promote the balanced development of natural sign language and sign Chinese.

  • Exploring Thematic Implication of The Invisible Man from Language Art (On British Literature)
    H.G. Wells' novel *The Invisible Man* explores the dangers of isolation and uncontrolled technological progress through its gothic language style and rich psychological descriptions. I delve into the symbolic meanings represented by the character of the Invisible Man, including bodily invisibility as a metaphor for social alienation and the inseparable relationship between humans and society, revealing the ethical dilemmas of scientific exploration and its profound effects on individual psychology.

  • Narrative point of view and symbolic implications in the Time Machine (On British Literature)
    The novel’s multi-layered narrative approach and the profound reflections on the future of the British Empire presented through these narrative techniques reveal the changes and challenges human civilization may face in its evolutionary progress and regress.

  • Reflection Essay on Bertrand Russell's A Free Man's Worship (On Philosophy and Modern English Essays)
    In this essay, I discuss Bertrand Russell's *A Free Man's Worship*, exploring his views on the meaning of life, particularly how humans find purpose and meaning in a cold, indifferent universe.

  • The Rationality of Dr. Faustus's Pursuit from Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs (On Psychology and Modern English Essays)
    Analyzing Marlowe's *The Tragedy History of Dr. Faustus* through Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs theory, this article explores the motivations and rationality behind Dr. Faustus's pursuit of knowledge and power. It interprets Dr. Faustus's actions from a psychological perspective, revealing his inner conflict and desire for self-actualization.

  • The Narration in The Murder of Roger Ackroyd The Narrator in "The Murder of Roger Ackroyd" (About British Literature)
    "The Murder of Roger Ackroyd", a famous work by Agatha Christie, is renowned for its unique narrative techniques and surprising plot twists in the mystery novel genre. This report is divided into three chapters. The first chapter introduces the novel and its author, as well as the definition of unreliable narration. The second chapter focuses on the specific manifestation and function of unreliable narration in the novel. The third chapter reflects on some controversies surrounding this novel and presents my conclusions.

  • Behind the “bad guys” Behind the “Bad Guys” (About British Literature)
    By analyzing "bad guy" characters in literary works, such as Shylock in "The Merchant of Venice," Professor Moriarty in "Sherlock Holmes," and Judge Wargrave in "And Then There Were None," this article explores the multifaceted humanity behind these characters. I argue that everyone may possess both good and bad traits. The round characters in literary works showcase this complexity, revealing the intricate nature of human personalities and motivations, and reminding us to approach others with tolerance and understanding.

  • Exogenous Moral Education Based on Practice and Reflection (About Class Teachers and Moral Education)
    Moral education is an important component of school education. While the development of moral education in schools is steadily advancing, moral deficiencies still exist in the results, and the national moral standards still need improvement. There are also areas of moral education that are being neglected. Implementing exogenous moral education based on practice and reflection aims to focus on helping students construct correct moral views through independent practice and reflection, breaking free from rigid theoretical knowledge, and enabling students to internally align with universally accepted social moral standards.

  • British Tipping Etiquette British Tipping Etiquette (About British Society and Culture)
    This article studies the culture of tipping in the UK, exploring how to properly tip in different situations and circumstances. It examines the historical origins of tipping, who to tip, how much to tip, and tipping customs in various contexts, as well as comparing tipping practices in the UK with those of other countries. Additionally, the article investigates British attitudes toward service charges, finding that many people dislike automatic tips and prefer to give cash tips directly.
