About My Blog

These are the articles, reports, and shares I have written in recent years, reflecting my daily academic thoughts and reflections, mainly focused on discussions of English and American literature and pedagogy. I believe these academic explorations are valuable, and I share them here with teachers and friends around me.

For reprints of the original content, please cite the source.


2023

  • Exploring the Impact of Kinesthetic Experiences and Spatial Metaphors in Teaching Practice on Second Grade Students' Comprehension of Abstract Verbs (About children's language acquisition)
    A long article exploring the effectiveness of two cognitive theories — embodied cognition theory and conceptual metaphor theory — in teaching abstract verbs. By integrating kinesthetic experiences and spatial metaphors into teaching, the study evaluates the impact of these innovative teaching methods on second-grade students' understanding of abstract verbs, aiming to improve language learning outcomes and promote children's language and cognitive development. (Co-author: Xia Hongyu)

  • Report: The item-based nature of children's early syntactic development - Slides | Handouts (About children's language acquisition)
    This report introduces Tomasello's theory of child language acquisition. Children learn concrete linguistic expressions from the language they hear around them through imitation, and then use their general cognitive and social cognitive skills to categorize, generalize, and creatively combine these individually learned expressions and structures. Where do grammatical structures come from? — In general, we can hypothesize that human language ultimately originated from a species-specific biological adaptation for symbolic communication, but the actual grammatical structure of modern languages was created by humans through specific cultural historical grammaticalization processes, as well as through cultural learning, schema formation, and structural combination in individual development.

  • A Comparative Analysis of Tragic Romance in The Scarlet Letter and A Rose for Emily (About American literature)
    This paper explores the tragic love stories of Hester Prynne in Nathaniel Hawthorne's The Scarlet Letter and Emily Grierson in William Faulkner's A Rose for Emily, aiming to explain the common elements and unique characteristics of their love stories. The analysis reveals the profound impacts of social constraints, differing views on love, and their respective coping mechanisms.

  • A Comparative Analysis of the Symbol of the Clothing in The Emperor's New Clothes and The Happy Man's Shirt (About western children's literature)
    This article compares Hans Christian Andersen's The Emperor's New Clothes and Italo Calvino's The Happy Man's Shirt, focusing on how they use clothing as a symbolic medium to convey themes of power, desire, materialism, oppression by the ruling class, and resistance by new forces. I analyze the vivid metaphors, symbolism, and social criticism within these stories, revealing the profound moral messages hidden beneath seemingly simple plots.

  • The development and predicament of Chinese sign language (About linguistics)
    Explores the standardization process of Chinese Sign Language and the challenges it faces, analyzing the differences between natural sign language and sign Chinese, as well as the difficulties in applying it in deaf education. Despite some achievements in standardization, language differences persist in practical teaching, and it is recommended that future education pay more attention to the needs of deaf students, promoting the balanced development of natural sign language and sign Chinese.

  • Exploring Thematic Implication of The Invisible Man from Language Art (About British literature)
    H.G. Wells' novel The Invisible Man explores the dangers of isolation and unchecked technological advancement through its Gothic language style and rich psychological descriptions. I delve into the symbolic meaning of the character of the Invisible Man, including bodily invisibility as a metaphor for social alienation, and the inseparable relationship between humanity and society, revealing the ethical dilemmas of scientific exploration and its profound impact on individual psychology.

  • Narrative point of view and symbolic implications in the Time Machine (About British literature)
    This novel presents a multi-layered narrative approach and offers a profound reflection on the future of the British Empire. It reveals the potential changes and challenges human civilization may face through evolution and regression.

  • Reflection Essay on Bertrand Russell's A Free Man's Worship (About philosophy and modern English prose)
    In this essay, I discuss Bertrand Russell's A Free Man's Worship, exploring his views on the meaning of life, particularly how humans find purpose and meaning in a cold and indifferent universe.

  • The Rationality of Dr. Faustus's Pursuit from Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs (About psychology and modern English prose)
    This article analyzes the character of Faust in Goethe's iconic work Dr. Faustus, exploring his motivations and the rationality behind his pursuit of knowledge and power. The essay interprets Faust's actions from a psychological perspective, uncovering his internal conflicts and his desire for self-actualization.

  • The Narration in The Murder of Roger Ackroyd (About British literature)
    The Murder of Roger Ackroyd, a famous work by Agatha Christie, is renowned for its unique narrative technique and surprising plot in the mystery novel genre. This report is divided into three chapters. The first chapter introduces the novel and the author, and defines unreliable narration. The second chapter focuses on the specific manifestations and functions of unreliable narration in the novel. The third chapter reflects on some controversies regarding this novel and my conclusion.

  • Behind the “bad guys” (About British literature)
    By analyzing the "bad guy" characters in several literary works, such as Shylock in The Merchant of Venice, Professor Moriarty in Sherlock Holmes, and Judge Wargrave in And Then There Were None, this piece explores the multidimensional humanity behind these "villains". I believe that everyone can embody both good and bad traits, and the round characters in literature demonstrate this point, revealing the complexity of human personality and behavioral motives. It reminds us to maintain tolerance and understanding when judging others.

  • Practice and Reflection-Based Indigenous Moral Education (About class teacher and moral education work)
    Moral education is an important part of school education. Although moral education construction in schools is progressing steadily, the results still reveal a lack of morality, and the national moral level still needs improvement. There are still neglected aspects of moral education in schools. Conducting practice and reflection-based indigenous moral education focuses on allowing students to construct a correct moral outlook through autonomous practice and reflection, breaking the constraints of rigid theoretical knowledge, enabling the educated to achieve a high internal recognition of the common moral standards of society.

  • British Tipping Etiquette (About British society and culture)
    This research explores the tipping culture in the UK, learning how to tip appropriately in different situations. It examines the historical origins of tipping, who to tip, how much to tip, and the tipping habits in various contexts, as well as comparing tipping practices in the UK with those of other countries. In addition, it explores the British attitude towards service charges, finding that many people are opposed to automatically added tips and prefer to give tips directly in cash.
